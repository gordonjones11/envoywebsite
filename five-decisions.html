The Intelligent Factory:   The Five Decisions

Five decisions that run every factory.

Strip away the org charts and the technology, and running a mill comes down to five decisions — made over and over, every shift, by people working with incomplete information. Intelligence doesn't replace these decisions. It improves their quality and consistency.

The Framework

The decisions that shape every shift.

The framework works across mills, plants, and other process operations. A paper mill may call the first decision machine speed; a specialty chemicals plant may call it feed rate or batch cycle time. The decision logic is the same: leaders must decide how to run the operation under real operating and market conditions. Each decision is explored in depth below — and each returns throughout Leading the Intelligent Factory.

1

How fast should we run?

The operating-rate decision — balancing throughput, yield, quality, reliability, energy, equipment health, and market conditions. The intelligent factory doesn't simply run faster; it learns when to push, when to hold, and when to back off.

2

How should we balance the process?

Every unit operation affects the next. The factory performs as a system, not a collection of isolated assets — and one area winning at the expense of the whole is the most common way mills lose.

3

When should we intervene?

Act too early and you chase noise; too late and a drift becomes a break. The intelligent factory improves the timing, proportionality, and credibility of intervention — across every shift.

4

What should we fix first?

Maintenance hours and capital are always scarcer than the list of things that need them. Prioritizing by risk, value, and constraint impact — not squeaky wheels — changes what a mill achieves with the same budget.

5

What can we learn from yesterday?

Every shift generates lessons; few mills capture them. Turning yesterday's upsets, saves, and successes into workflows, runbooks, and standards is how good operations become great ones.

Decision 1

How fast should we run?

The intelligent factory knows when to push, when to hold, and when to back off.

The decision

Every factory has to decide how hard to push the operation. This is not simply a speed question — it is a rate-setting decision that balances throughput, yield, quality, reliability, energy, safety, equipment health, constraints, customer demand, margin, inventory position, and profit per hour. In a paper mill, it's deciding how fast to run the machine on a given grade based on moisture profile, sheet break risk, dryer limitations, recent instability, and order demand.

What makes it hard

Operating rate is a commercial decision as much as a production decision. When the market will absorb everything the mill can make at attractive margins, the right move may be to push toward demonstrated capability while managing quality and reliability risk. When the market is constrained, running flat out creates excess inventory, wastes energy, and stresses equipment — the intelligent decision may be to optimize yield, energy, quality, grade mix, or cash contribution instead of maximum throughput. Intelligence improves the decision by connecting current operating conditions with historical performance, known constraints, quality risk, maintenance risk, and economic impact.

Key Questions

Before you push the rate

  • Is the market asking us to maximize output, or are we under demand, margin, or inventory constraints?
  • What is the current constraint — process, equipment, utilities, materials, downstream capacity, or market demand?
  • What happens to quality, yield, energy, and downtime risk as rate increases?
  • Is the operation stable enough to push? Are we carrying unresolved maintenance or reliability risk?
  • Would running slightly slower produce more good output, better yield, or better profit per hour?
  • Are different shifts, grades, or lines running at different rates for good reasons?

Decision 2

How should we balance the process?

Local optimization is not the same as factory optimization.

The decision

Every factory has to balance flows, constraints, setpoints, utilities, energy, quality, inventory, and production commitments across interconnected process areas. In a paper mill, that means balancing wet-end stability, machine speed, press loading, dryer capacity, steam use, moisture targets, sheet break risk, broke handling, and order demand — all at once. The factory performs as a system, not as a collection of isolated assets.

What makes it hard

The local-optimization trap: a mill can make one area look better while making the total operation worse. One line, unit, department, or KPI improves while throughput, quality, energy, reliability, or profit per hour deteriorates somewhere else. Most mills manage this balance on instinct. The intelligent factory makes system-level tradeoffs visible — helping leaders and frontline teams understand how changes in one part of the operation affect the rest — so no part of the factory wins at the expense of the whole.

Key Questions

Seeing the whole system

  • Where is the current constraint — and is it fixed, or moving?
  • Which upstream and downstream areas are affecting each other?
  • Are we optimizing one asset, line, or KPI at the expense of the total process?
  • What setpoints or operating choices are driving quality variation, instability, waste, or energy use?
  • Are inventory, WIP, tank levels, or broke creating hidden constraints?
  • Which balance produces the best total result across throughput, yield, quality, energy, reliability, and profit per hour?

Decision 3

When should we intervene?

Intervene at the right time, for the right reason, with the right level of response.

The decision

Every factory has to decide when a signal, trend, or abnormal condition has crossed the threshold from watch to action. This is not only a control-room decision — it can be an operator adjustment, an engineering investigation, a maintenance inspection, a quality hold, a rate change, or a leadership escalation. In a mill, it's deciding when moisture variation, vibration, sheet instability, or a steam imbalance deserves action before it becomes a break or a quality issue.

What makes it hard

The timing tradeoff cuts both ways. Late intervention lets small abnormal conditions become downtime, quality loss, equipment damage, or customer impact. Over-intervention creates alarm fatigue, unnecessary work, operator frustration, and loss of trust. Intelligence improves this decision by distinguishing normal variation from meaningful change and guiding the right level of response — because early warning is only valuable when it leads to timely, proportional, and trusted action.

Framework

The Intervention Ladder

Seven rungs, from watching to learning:

  1. Monitor — the signal is visible, but no action is required yet
  2. Investigate — the pattern is unusual enough to check context and likely causes
  3. Adjust — a controlled operating change is appropriate
  4. Escalate — the issue requires engineering, maintenance, quality, or leadership involvement
  5. Contain — the risk is high enough to protect safety, quality, equipment, or production continuity
  6. Verify — confirm whether the intervention worked
  7. Learn — update the workflow, threshold, runbook, or operating practice

Decision 4

What should we fix first?

The loudest problem is not always the most important problem.

The decision

Every factory has more problems, risks, and opportunities than resources to address them. And “fix” means more than repairing equipment — it can mean fixing an asset, a process condition, a quality driver, a reliability pattern, a workflow, an information gap, or a management-system gap: the signal no one owns, the alert no one closes, the recurring issue that never becomes standard work. In a mill, it's choosing between a dryer limitation, a recurring sheet break pattern, a critical pump vibration, a bad actor valve, and a quality variation on a high-margin grade.

What makes it hard

Priority is not the same as urgency. Something can feel urgent because it is recent, visible, loud, or politically important — but the right priority depends on risk, value, constraint impact, customer impact, recurrence, and actionability. Safety, regulatory, and environmental obligations are never just another economic tradeoff; existing compliance systems remain authoritative. Once those are respected, intelligence helps leaders focus scarce resources on the problems that matter most. A signal without prioritization becomes backlog.

Framework

The Fix-First Filter

Seven tests for what deserves attention now:

  1. Risk — what could happen if we do nothing?
  2. Value — how much performance, margin, quality, energy, or production is at stake?
  3. Constraint — does this affect the current or future bottleneck?
  4. Customer — could this affect quality, delivery, or customer commitments?
  5. Urgency — how soon does action need to happen?
  6. Actionability — can we do something useful with the people, parts, and window we have?
  7. Recurrence — will fixing this remove a repeating loss, or only treat today's symptom?

Decision 5

What can we learn from yesterday?

Reporting tells you what happened. Learning changes what happens next.

The decision

Every factory has to decide what to remember, change, standardize, or improve based on recent events — and “yesterday” means the last shift, the last grade change, the last upset, the last maintenance job, or the last month of recurring losses. In a mill, it's learning from a sheet break, a moisture variation, or a steam imbalance so the next similar event is diagnosed faster and handled more consistently.

What makes it hard

Looking backward is not the same as learning. Mills already have production reports, downtime reports, shift logs, maintenance histories, and dashboards — but those artifacts only become learning when they change what people do next. Too much knowledge stays trapped in shift notes, maintenance comments, engineering spreadsheets, and individual memory, and recurring problems get solved from scratch again and again. A factory has not learned from an event until the learning changes a workflow, runbook, threshold, standard, model, or training material.

Framework

The Yesterday-to-Tomorrow Loop

Seven steps that turn experience into advantage:

  1. Capture — what happened?
  2. Contextualize — what was the operating situation?
  3. Explain — why did it happen, or what are the likely causes?
  4. Decide — what should we do differently?
  5. Update — which workflow, runbook, threshold, standard, or training material should change?
  6. Share — who needs to know before the issue repeats?
  7. Verify — did the change improve future performance?
